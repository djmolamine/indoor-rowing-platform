## How to use these personas

These are decision tools, not demographic stereotypes. Teams should test major flows against at least the first four athlete personas and one partner persona. No persona may become the default at the expense of machine independence, unaffiliated athletes, accessibility, or privacy.

## 1. The independent home rower

### Context

Trains at home three to five times per week. May own a WaterRower, Concept2, RP3, or lower-cost Bluetooth machine. Has no club and may not identify as a competitive rower.

### Goals

- Maintain a complete record.
- See consistency and improvement.
- Stay motivated through achievable missions.
- Feel part of indoor rowing without joining a club.

### Frictions

- Manufacturer app feels isolated or limited.
- Machine may not export rich data.
- Rankings dominated by incompatible equipment feel unfair.
- Community experiences can feel designed only for racers.

### Product response

Fast manual/OCR/Bluetooth capture, machine-class-aware progress, optional community, River Expeditions, private-by-default Passport, and no club requirement.

## 2. The commercial-gym athlete

### Context

Rows on shared Technogym, Matrix, Concept2, or other machines. Equipment and connectivity vary by location. Sessions are often short and logged from a phone after training.

### Goals

- Capture workouts quickly on shared equipment.
- Avoid losing history when changing gyms.
- Compare sessions despite inconsistent machines.
- Discover challenges that accept available equipment.

### Frictions

- Cannot permanently pair a machine.
- Photos may be the only record.
- Machine model is sometimes unknown.
- Gym privacy and connectivity are inconsistent.

### Product response

Excellent monitor-photo flow, reusable gym/machine memory, offline-friendly drafts, confidence labels, provider-neutral history, and clear event eligibility.

## 3. The performance-focused multi-machine athlete

### Context

Trains with structured sessions at home and in a club or gym. Uses more than one machine and cares about pace, watts, stroke rate, intervals, heart rate, and personal records.

### Goals

- Consolidate detailed data.
- Separate comparable from non-comparable efforts.
- Track seasons and personal bests.
- Enter trusted rankings and competitions.

### Frictions

- Data schemas and export quality differ.
- Simple dashboards hide provenance.
- Cross-machine leaderboards can be misleading.
- Editing or duplicate behavior can undermine trust.

### Product response

Rich canonical workout data, interval preservation, provenance, machine classes, verification tiers, audit history, advanced filters, and export.

## 4. The competition athlete

### Context

Plans around virtual or physical events. May have age, gender or competition-category, weight, adaptive, federation, or club eligibility considerations.

### Goals

- Find appropriate events.
- Understand eligibility and deadlines.
- Submit verifiable results.
- Maintain a credible competition history.

### Frictions

- Event information is fragmented.
- Registration and results use different systems.
- Rules and machine requirements are unclear.
- Official messages may be missed or mixed with marketing.

### Product response

Verified event pages, saved-event reminders, explicit machine/rule requirements, event-specific consent, structured verification, result appeals, and Passport credentials.

## 5. The club athlete

### Context

Belongs to a rowing club, university, school, CrossFit box, gym team, or virtual crew. Club membership enhances identity but is not the whole identity.

### Goals

- Contribute meters and results to the group.
- Join club challenges.
- Compare fairly within relevant groups.
- Share selected workouts with coaches or teammates.

### Frictions

- A person may change clubs or have multiple roles.
- Team challenge membership differs from long-term affiliation.
- Privacy expectations vary.

### Product response

Dated, role-based relationships; multiple memberships where rules allow; separate team and club concepts; permissioned coach access; independent participation preserved.

## 6. The returning or beginner athlete

### Context

Has limited rowing vocabulary, inconsistent training, or a long gap. Motivation is health, recovery, general fitness, or curiosity rather than competition.

### Goals

- Understand basic metrics.
- Build a sustainable habit.
- Avoid intimidating comparisons.
- Celebrate attainable progress.

### Frictions

- Pace and split terminology can be confusing.
- Elite rankings can feel irrelevant.
- Streak mechanics can punish normal interruptions.

### Product response

Plain-language education, gentle onboarding, consistency views, private goals, inclusive challenges, River Expeditions, and opt-in competition.

## 7. The adaptive athlete

### Context

May compete within established adaptive categories or row for fitness with modified equipment and individualized metrics.

### Goals

- Participate with dignity and accurate categorization.
- Find eligible events.
- Control sensitive classification information.
- Compare within meaningful rules.

### Frictions

- Categories differ by event and authority.
- Accessibility details may be unavailable.
- Sensitive information can be overexposed.

### Product response

Accessible design, event-owned classification rules, minimum disclosure, private eligibility credentials, adaptable machine metadata, and consultation with adaptive athletes.

## 8. The coach

### Context

Supports athletes individually or through a club. Needs permissioned visibility, not ownership of athlete accounts.

### Goals

- Review relevant workouts and adherence.
- Understand source quality.
- Organize groups and challenges.
- Prepare athletes for events.

### Frictions

- Athletes use different machines.
- Shared credentials and uncontrolled access are unsafe.
- Data overload makes review difficult.

### Product response

Explicit coach-athlete relationships, scoped permissions, revocation, comparable summaries, notes with clear ownership, and no silent access to private fields.

## 9. The event organizer

### Context

Runs a local race, virtual series, national championship, school event, charity challenge, or hybrid competition.

### Goals

- Publish authoritative information.
- Reach eligible interested athletes.
- Receive clean entries and results.
- Verify, correct, and publish outcomes.

### Frictions

- Spreadsheet and email operations do not scale.
- Machine and category rules are difficult to communicate.
- Identity matching and disputes consume time.

### Product response

Verified organization identity, structured events, rule templates, consented communications, result workflows, audits, and exports.

## 10. The machine provider

### Context

Manufactures equipment or operates a connected workout service. Wants athletes to value its machines and data without losing provider attribution.

### Goals

- Integrate reliably.
- Preserve brand and source attribution.
- Participate in compatible challenges and events.
- Receive integration health insights.

### Frictions

- Fear of becoming commoditized.
- Different data semantics.
- Security, support, and versioning costs.

### Product response

Clear adapter contracts, provider attribution, capability declarations, certification, sandbox testing, no claim of false comparability, and a fair partnership model.

## 11. The federation partner

### Context

A national federation or potential World Rowing stakeholder seeking participation growth, event support, athlete communication, and governance.

### Goals

- Reach consented athletes.
- Promote official pathways.
- Operate trusted competitions.
- Understand aggregate participation.

### Frictions

- Incomplete digital reach.
- Data-protection and safeguarding duties.
- Risk of confusing official and unofficial communications.

### Product response

Verified channels, role-based administration, consent enforcement, audit logs, privacy-safe aggregate reporting, explicit official status, and platform independence. See [15_WORLD_ROWING.md](15_WORLD_ROWING.md).
